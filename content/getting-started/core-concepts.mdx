# Core Concepts

Understand how autonomous agents work and learn to trust them with your most important tasks.

## What Makes an Agent Autonomous?

Think of an autonomous agent like a highly capable assistant who doesn't need constant supervision. You give them a goal, and they figure out how to achieve it.

### Agents vs. Simple Chatbots

A regular chatbot answers questions. An autonomous agent *does things*.

**Chatbot:** "What's on my calendar today?" → Lists your meetings

**Agent:** "Prepare me for my day" → Checks calendar, pulls relevant documents, summarizes overnight emails, drafts responses to urgent items, and briefs you on key priorities

The difference isn't just capability—it's initiative. Agents don't wait for step-by-step instructions. They understand your objective and chart their own path.

### Goal-Directed Behavior

Give an agent an objective, and it breaks it down into steps automatically.

**You say:** "Research our top five competitors and summarize their pricing."

**The agent figures out:**
1. Which competitors to research (or asks for clarification)
2. Where to find pricing information
3. How to handle incomplete or hidden pricing
4. What format would be most useful for you
5. When to flag something unusual

You focus on the *what*. The agent handles the *how*.

### Tool Use

Agents can take real actions in the world through integrations:

- **Read and write** to your Google Drive, Notion, or CRM
- **Send messages** through Slack or email
- **Search the web** for current information
- **Query databases** and APIs
- **Create documents** and reports

An agent without tools is just a conversationalist. An agent *with* tools is a productivity multiplier.

### Memory

Effective agents remember what matters. They maintain context across conversations, recall your preferences, and learn from past interactions.

**Short-term memory:** "You asked about the Acme deal yesterday—here's an update."

**Long-term memory:** "Based on your past preferences, I'll format this as a bullet list and email it to your team."

This continuity makes agents feel less like software and more like a colleague who knows how you work.

### Self-Correction

When something doesn't work, agents adapt.

**A rigid automation:** API returns unexpected data → workflow fails → you debug

**An autonomous agent:** API returns unexpected data → agent tries alternative approach → if still stuck, asks for help or works around it

This resilience is what lets you trust agents with complex tasks. They don't break at the first surprise.

## Observing Agent Behavior

Autonomy without visibility is just a black box. Scout gives you full transparency into what your agents are doing and why.

### Activity Logs

Every action your agent takes is logged. See at a glance:

- When the agent ran
- What tasks it completed
- How long each step took
- Whether it succeeded or encountered issues

This isn't just debugging—it's accountability. You should never wonder "what did my agent do all day?"

### Execution Traces

Follow the reasoning path:

- **Which tools were called** and in what order
- **What decisions were made** at each step
- **Why** the agent chose one approach over another

Think of it like a thought transcript. When an agent says "I couldn't find pricing for Competitor X," you can see exactly where it looked and what it found instead.

### Tool Usage

Know exactly what data your agent accessed:

- **Reads:** Which documents, databases, or APIs were queried
- **Writes:** What was created, updated, or sent
- **External calls:** Which third-party services were contacted

This visibility is essential for security and compliance—but also for understanding your agent's work.

### Error Handling

When things go wrong, observe how your agent responds:

- Did it retry? How many times?
- Did it try an alternative approach?
- Did it escalate to you or continue with partial information?

Healthy agents fail gracefully. They don't hide errors or pretend everything worked.

### Transparency

The ultimate question: *Why did my agent do that?*

With Scout, you can always find out. Every decision is traceable. Every action is logged. You're not trusting a mystery—you're trusting a system you can inspect.

## Using Agents Day-to-Day

Autonomy doesn't mean "set and forget." It means productive partnership. Here's how to work effectively with autonomous agents.

### Chat Interface

The simplest pattern: just talk to your agent.

**Conversational tasks:**
- "What did the product team decide yesterday?"
- "Draft a response to this customer complaint"
- "Find me all contracts expiring this quarter"

Chat is ideal for ad-hoc questions, one-off tasks, and collaborative problem-solving. You iterate together, refining until you get what you need.

### Scheduled Runs

Let your agent work while you sleep. Schedule it to run automatically:

- **Daily briefing** every morning at 8 AM
- **Weekly report** every Monday
- **Hourly monitoring** for critical systems

The agent wakes up, does its job, and delivers results—without you lifting a finger. [Learn more about scheduling](/agents/scheduling).

### Triggers

Agents can respond to events automatically:

- **New email** from a key client → agent drafts a response
- **Calendar event** created → agent prepares briefing materials
- **Slack mention** → agent jumps in to help
- **Form submission** → agent processes and routes

Triggers make agents proactive, not just reactive. They're working even when you're not asking.

### Delegation

Specialized agents can work together:

**You:** "Research Acme Corp and prepare for my sales call"

**Main agent delegates to:**
- Research Agent → Gathers company info and news
- CRM Agent → Pulls interaction history
- Writing Agent → Drafts the briefing document

Each agent focuses on what it does best. The result is higher quality than any single agent could produce alone. [Learn more about delegation](/agents/delegation).

### Human-in-the-Loop

Not every decision should be autonomous. Configure when your agent should:

**Act immediately:**
- Routine information gathering
- Low-risk operations
- Time-sensitive tasks

**Ask for approval:**
- Sending external communications
- Modifying important records
- Spending money or committing resources

The right balance depends on your comfort level. Start conservative, then expand autonomy as trust builds.

## Building Trust with Your Agent

Trust isn't automatic—it's earned. Here's how to develop confidence in your autonomous agents.

### Start Small

Begin with low-stakes tasks:

- Summarizing documents
- Researching public information
- Organizing data you already have

These tasks prove the agent's capability without risk. As you see consistent results, gradually increase complexity.

### Review Logs Regularly

In the beginning, check what your agent did after every run:

- Did it complete the task?
- Were there any surprising choices?
- Did it access only what it needed?

Over time, you'll develop intuition for when something needs attention versus when you can trust the agent handled it.

### Set Clear Boundaries

Limit sensitive operations until you're confident:

- Start with read-only access to critical systems
- Require approval for external communications
- Exclude sensitive folders or databases from agent access

You can always expand access later. It's harder to undo a mistake.

### Iterate on Instructions

Your agent's instructions are like onboarding a new employee. The first draft won't be perfect.

**Initial instruction:** "Research competitors and send me a report"

**After observing output:** "Research our top 5 competitors by market share. Focus on pricing changes in the last quarter. Format as a summary table with links to sources. Send to my email, don't post to Slack."

Each iteration makes the agent more effective at delivering what you actually want.

### Define Escalation Rules

Make the implicit explicit:

- "If you can't find information after 10 minutes, ask me for direction"
- "If a task would modify production data, require my approval"
- "If you detect anything unusual, notify me immediately rather than proceeding"

Clear rules prevent agents from getting stuck or making assumptions you wouldn't want.

## Key Concepts Summary

### Instructions

The natural language "programming" of your agent. Write instructions like you're briefing a smart colleague: clear goals, relevant context, and any constraints. [Learn more](/agents/getting-started).

### Tools & Integrations

What your agent can access and do. Connect your existing tools—Slack, Google Workspace, CRM, databases—and your agent gains the ability to read, write, search, and act. [Browse integrations](https://scoutos.com/integrations).

### Context

The information your agent works with. This includes:

- **Drive:** Files and documents the agent can access
- **Collections:** Structured data for search and retrieval
- **Conversations:** Previous interactions for continuity

Good context makes for good outputs.

### Scheduling

When your agent runs automatically. Daily check-ins, weekly reports, or custom timing—the choice is yours. The agent wakes up, executes, and delivers results on your schedule. [Learn more](/agents/scheduling).

### Observability

How you monitor and understand agent behavior. Logs, traces, and tool usage give you complete visibility into what your agent did and why. Essential for trust, debugging, and compliance.
